# #EuropeTravelTips — The Eurail Pass explained 🚄 (a money-saving tip, not a travel story)

source: https://medium.com/@yiiihua_0319/b8b4a3041bb8
published: Feb 27, 2024

A rail pass covering 33 European countries — think of it as a much bigger JR Pass. The app is Rail Planner: buy it, activate it, get on the train. I've bought one three times and love it.

## Should you buy one?
1. You're visiting more than three countries (for one or two, check whether that country has its own pass — Germany and Switzerland do)
2. Train fares there are expensive. For example, the 15-days-in-2-months youth second class pass works out at about NT$800 a day, while a single from Manchester to London is £30+ — one journey and it's paid for itself
3. Your plans are loose: activate it and get on, with no fear of last-minute fare hikes

## Types of pass
- Age: adult / youth (12–27) / children 4–12 free (two per adult) / under 4 free
- Format: continuous (every day within the period) or flexi (any N days within the period, slightly more expensive)
- Class: first (roomier, less crowded, sometimes with food) or second (perfectly comfortable)

## What's good about it
- Unlimited travel in a day: cross-border trains sometimes need a €6–8 seat reservation. I once did Vienna → the airport and then London airport → Manchester in one day; the tickets would have been over £50, but it averaged out to NT$800 for the day
- You can ride Germany's fastest ICE trains
- Activate any time: even deciding to leave tomorrow, there's no price surge

## What's not
- No live train information (delays and cancellations are common in Germany and the UK, so pair it with the local app for platforms and cancellations)
- No refund when a train is delayed
