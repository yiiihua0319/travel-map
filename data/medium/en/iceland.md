Hello everyone, I'm Eva 🌸 This time it's Iceland, a place out of a dream, and these eight days were the happiest week of my whole exchange year. If you'd rather watch it, the vlog is on my YouTube channel under #IcelandVlog

## Getting around

Iceland is big and public transport is limited, so we drove the whole way. The only public transport we used was from the airport into Reykjavík, where you can take either a private coach or the public bus. The private coach was about £20 for a 45-minute ride — outrageously expensive! The public bus is quite a bit cheaper (four or five times cheaper). If you're going any distance, rent a car.

Most rental companies here include an airport transfer. We used Lotus, which also throws in coffee vouchers for N1 service stations and 3 ISK off per litre of fuel.

From 17:00 on 3/1 to 17:00 on 3/8, one car came to 110,322 ISK (about NT$25,574) including full insurance — which covers your car door being blown off, incidentally. We paid about NT$2,400 more over the eight days to add a second driver (if someone other than the named renter is driving, the insurance won't pay out).

Eight of us took two Suzuki Vitaras, four to a car. We'd rented small cars and everyone had a suitcase, so we were worried it wouldn't all fit — it just about did 😂 Something to keep in mind if you're planning this.

## Food

Iceland is expensive, roughly double the UK, and fresh fruit and vegetables are astronomical. So we cooked for all eight days and only ate out once, for lobster.

📍 Pakkhús Restaurant (Höfn)
The lobster restaurant is in the little town of Höfn, where almost everyone heading east stays a night (it's close to the glacier hike and the blue ice cave). Book ahead. Unanimously loved by all of us! The lobster was tender and fresh, and the lamb came from sheep that had grazed outdoors for eight months — tender and not at all gamey. The lobster soup was a touch salty, which was just right with bread to dip. The large lobster and the lobster soup are the ones to get 🦞
Prices: large lobster 8,990 ISK, butter lobster 9,500 ISK, lamb chops 6,550 ISK, lobster soup 2,700 ISK

📍 Sandholt (Reykjavík)
Excellent bakery in the city. 650 ISK apiece, and the Danish pastry is genuinely wonderful — the kind you want to carry back to England 🍞

## Supermarket shopping

Two supermarkets are worth your time; everywhere else costs double!
- Costco: bring your membership card (a Taiwanese one works). It's the only branch, sitting between the airport and Reykjavík, so shop on your way in from the plane
- Bónus: the cheapest chain, with far more branches in the west than the east. If you're heading east, stock up first

While you're in Bónus, buy the famous Icelandic chocolate — I compared prices everywhere and this is the cheapest (souvenir shops charge nearly 800 ISK, here it's 398 ISK)

If you're coming from Taiwan, bring plenty of instant noodles, or bring sauces and buy the noodles there

## The route

Day 1: KEF airport ➡️ our guesthouse in the city ➡️ Northern Light View Point (my first time ever seeing the aurora, a faint green)

Day 2: Hvalfjörður ➡️ Glanni Waterfall (free) ➡️ shopping at Bónus ➡️ sunset at Kirkjufell ➡️ Northern Light View Point (much clearer this time, everyone got a photo with the aurora)
In March the sun sets around 7pm in Iceland, and it turns the sky a dreamy pink

Day 3: Kerið crater lake ➡️ the Golden Circle (Geysir and Gullfoss are essential) ➡️ Þingvellir National Park ➡️ Öxarárfoss
Kerið costs 500 ISK; in winter the lake is frozen, and at the top the wind is fierce and the ground slippery, so take care. Personally I think Þingvellir is better in summer — in winter the waterfalls and lakes are all frozen. Our tyre pressure dropped on the way; petrol stations have free air pumps. Try to stay further east, or you'll be driving five hours the next morning to the glacier hike meeting point

Day 4: five hours east ➡️ 3.5-hour glacier hike
The meeting point is near Skaftafell. The guide provides a waterproof jacket, boots and crampons. Walking on a thousand-year-old glacier and seeing the natural ice caves isn't as tiring as it sounds. NT$4,352 and worth every bit of it. Watch out for speed cameras, the fines are steep. We stayed in Höfn that night

Day 5: blue ice cave ➡️ Jökulsárlón glacier lagoon ➡️ Diamond Beach ➡️ lobster dinner at Pakkhús Restaurant
The blue ice cave is 2.5 hours and €145.25 (about NT$5,000). You ride in by jeep for 30 minutes, and inside it's like the ice world in Frozen 🩵 magical. It's much easier than the glacier hike (60% of it is taking photos) — a pregnant woman could do the whole thing. Take your photos early on, as the cave gets darker further in

Day 6: Skútafoss ➡️ Sveinsstekksfoss ➡️ shopping at Bónus ➡️ the town from The Secret Life of Walter Mitty
Heading northeast the roads start to ice over ❄️ so drive carefully ⚠️ On the way you pass the stretch of road where he skateboards in the film, and the view down over the town is beautiful

Day 7: the Walter Mitty town ➡️ Skógafoss ➡️ Seljalandsfoss ➡️ back to Reykjavík (a long 7–8 hour push)

Day 8: Hallgrímskirkja (about 220 ISK to go up the tower for the city view) ➡️ Rainbow Street ➡️ KEF airport
For souvenirs in the city I'd suggest 📍 Iceland Memories (cheapest fridge magnets) and 📍 Sandholt for bread. We saw the aurora from the plane — a tip: flying south, sit on the east side for a chance at it

## Things to know

- Some national parks and car parks charge. Pay before you leave or you may be fined — there are cameras everywhere
- Icelandic fines are steep. Someone in our group was doing just over 100 in a 90 zone and was fined 20,650 ISK; pay within a month for a 25% discount, by bank transfer to the account given
- March weather is unstable, the north is risky and there isn't much to see up there, so we skipped it. Summer suits the north better
- My regrets: never found the little red house they use for eye tests, and never made it into a hot spring ♨️ — reasons to go back

Iceland is beautiful enough to be worth a second trip 💕
